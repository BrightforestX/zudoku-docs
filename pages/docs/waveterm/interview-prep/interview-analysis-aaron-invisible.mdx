## 1. Company analysis (Invisible Technologies)

**Purpose:** A clear, multi-perspective mental model of the company so you can speak to their
strategy, constraints, risks, and why the role exists—and sound like someone who has done the
homework.

### What Invisible is

Invisible Technologies is an **AI software platform** (founded 2015) for the enterprise: data
cleaning and labeling for AI, model adaptation, and human-in-the-loop where it matters. They are
positioned as **"AI infrastructure for the enterprise"** and (per a16z) sit in the **"AI Palantir"**
category—"WeMakeAIReal." In **September 2025** they raised **$100M** in growth funding (led by
Vanara Capital), reaching a **$2B+ valuation**. They have been **profitable for 5+ years**, with
**~$134M revenue (2024)**, ranked **#2 in AI on the Inc. 5000** (overall #152), **~3,100
employees**, and **54% YoY growth**. This signals they are scaling fast and need people who can
operate in customer environments without constant hand-holding.

### Platform and product (with interview angles)

Five pillars:

- **Neuron** – Data infrastructure and ontologies; integrates and normalizes varied data sources for
  AI.
- **Atomic** – Process/workflow builder; codifies business logic and workflows so they can be
  automated and improved.
- **Axon** – Agentic engine; executes AI-driven processes with **full transparency and audit logs**
  (critical for government).
- **Synapse** – Evaluation platform; measures model performance, red-teams for reliability and bias.
- **Expert Marketplace** – Vetted trainers and human expertise; RLHF and secure model improvement.

**Interview angle:** Government messaging stresses **auditability, trust, and mission alignment**
over black-box AI. Your answers should mirror that: evidence for assessors, transparency,
human-in-the-loop where it matters. Mentioning **Axon's auditability** or **Synapse's red-teaming**
shows you've read the platform.

### Public sector bet (why they're hiring now)

In **October 2024** Invisible launched **Global Public Sector Operations**. **Wes Green** joined as
SVP, Global Public Sector (28 years defense and national security). **Sharon Woods** joined as SVP,
Enterprise Accounts, Public Sector—Aaron shared and congratulated her; public sector is a stated
focus. Use cases: fragmented legacy systems, sensitive data, **FedRAMP/compliance**, defense and
intelligence analysis, benefits processing, health data. **FDEs are the frontline** for these
engagements—so Aaron is hiring for a **strategic growth area**, not a backwater. Frame your interest
as aligned with that bet.

### Competitive and market perspective

"AI Palantir" framing means they compete on **enterprise trust**, data integration, and
**mission-critical deployments**—not just demos. They need engineers who think in terms of
**repeatable patterns, compliance evidence, and customer success**, not just feature velocity. A
candidate who only talks about moving fast will sound misaligned; one who talks about **"how this
lands at the next agency"** or **"evidence for the assessor"** sounds like a fit.

### Risks and pressures (what keeps leadership up at night)

Scaling public sector without blowing up on compliance or delivery; maintaining trust with
government customers; hiring enough people who can work in constraints. Your narrative—**production
readiness, compliance-by-design, handoff**—directly addresses these. Weave that into "why FDE" and
"why Invisible."

### Why this matters for the interview

Invisible needs people who can deploy and operate in **high-stakes, regulated environments**,
explain trade-offs to **non-technical mission owners**, and align with **audit and compliance** from
day one. Drop 2–3 company-specific phrases: **auditability**, **agents in the enterprise**, **public
sector bet**—so Aaron feels you understand where they're going.

## 3. What he will ask (predicted questions and rationale)

**Purpose:** Tie each predicted question to his motivations and to what a strong (or weak) answer
looks like—and prepare you for curveballs and follow-ups.

### Opening / fit

**Question:** "Tell me about yourself."

- **Why he's asking:** Narrative, relevance to FDE and public sector, one concrete technical win.
- **Strong answer:** 2–3 min: background → one production-ready or compliance-relevant example (e.g.
  [banking API](../banking-api-fde-narrative.md)) → why FDE / public sector / Invisible.
- **Red flag:** Generic, no link to regulated/production/compliance or to this role.

**Possible follow-up:** "Why leave your current role?" or "What would make you say no to this
role?"  
**Suggested framing:** Brief, positive (growth, mission, fit). Do not badmouth current employer or
sound hesitant.

---

### Behavioral (compliance, production, stakeholders, handoff)

Use **STAR** (Situation, Task, Action, Result) and end with a **lesson**. Be specific.

**Q1: Tell me about a time you had to build or operate a system under strict compliance (e.g. HIPAA,
SOC 2, FedRAMP, or government rules).**

- **Why he's asking:** Has you actually operated under real constraints and can you talk about
  controls and evidence?
- **Strong:** STAR with audit logging, access controls, change process; outcome (passed audit, no
  findings, or traceable controls); lesson: "Compliance is easier when it's part of the design and
  pipeline, not a bolt-on."
- **Red flag:** Vague, no framework named, no evidence or outcome.

**Q2: Tell me about a time you owned a system in production and had to respond to an incident or
outage.**

- **Why he's asking:** Ownership, triage, communication, learning from failure.
- **Strong:** What broke, how you found out; your role (triage, fix, communicate); what you did to
  reduce recurrence; takeaway: "Clear ownership and runbooks matter more when the customer is
  watching."
- **Red flag:** No outcome or no lesson; blame others.

**Q3: Tell me about a time you had to explain a technical trade-off to a non-technical
stakeholder.**

- **Why he's asking:** Can you translate technical choices into impact and options for
  decision-makers?
- **Strong:** Who they were, the trade-off (e.g. speed vs. security), how you framed it (impact on
  their goals, options, recommendation), aligned decision.
- **Red flag:** Only technical jargon; no clear outcome.

**Q4: Describe how you've handed off a system to a customer or ops team so they could run it without
you.**

- **Why he's asking:** FDE mindset—documentation, runbooks, sustainable handoff.
- **Strong:** What you built, who took it over; what you provided (runbooks, docs, training,
  escalation); how you validated (walkthrough, tabletop, first incident together); lesson on what
  makes handoffs stick.
- **Red flag:** No validation step or no clear owner.

**Q5: Why are you interested in Forward Deployed and the U.S. public sector specifically?**

- **Why he's asking:** Motivation and fit.
- **Strong:** **30–60 seconds**, rehearsed: mission impact, working in customer's environment,
  variety of problems, compliance and security as first-class; biggest challenges (long cycles,
  strict compliance, legacy) and how you'd mitigate (process, automation, documentation).
- **Red flag:** Generic or lukewarm; no link to public sector or constraints.

**Curveballs (with suggested answers):**

- **"What would you do differently?"** – "I'd [one concrete change]. The lesson I took was [one
  sentence]."
- **"What did you learn about yourself?"** – "I learned that I [strength or growth area] when [brief
  context]."
- **"How did you handle someone who disagreed?"** – "I listened to their concern, restated it to
  confirm, then we looked at the trade-offs together and [outcome]."

### Technical / system design

**Primary: Audit logging for a FedRAMP environment**

**How he might phrase it:** "Design an audit-logging system for an application that has to meet
FedRAMP Moderate. I care about what you log, how you retain and protect it, who can access it, and
how you'd prove compliance to an assessor."

- **Why he's asking:** Can you design for compliance and prove it to an assessor? Do you think in
  terms of controls and evidence?
- **Strong:** (1) **Clarifying questions:** event volume and retention; who accesses logs (ops vs.
  auditors); ATO boundary. (2) **High-level components:** ingest (structured events, no PII unless
  approved), store (durable, append-only, partition by time), retain (policy, automated
  deletion/archive), access control (authorized roles only; access to logs is itself logged;
  separation of duties). (3) **Immutability and integrity:** append-only, no in-place edit; optional
  hash chain or signed batches for assessor verification. (4) **Proving compliance:** Map to FedRAMP
  **AU family** (AU-2 audit events, AU-6 review/analysis, AU-9 protection of audit information,
  AU-12 audit generation). Provide runbooks, config, and control-to-implementation matrix for
  assessor. "I'd start from the **ATO boundary** and the AU control set, then design the pipeline so
  every requirement maps to a concrete component."
- **Red flag:** No mention of retention, access control, or how you'd demonstrate compliance; only
  technical detail with no compliance framing.

**Clarifying questions to ask:** (1) Expected event volume and retention? (2) Who needs to access
logs—internal ops only, or also auditors/security? (3) Are we in scope as the application provider,
or part of a larger boundary?

**Likely follow-ups (short answer guidance):**

- **"What if the customer is air-gapped?"** – Logs stay on-prem; ship a logging stack as part of the
  deployment artifact with same retention and access rules. Updates go through their change process
  (approved media or internal repo).
- **"How do you handle log volume at scale?"** – Partition by time and tenant; tier hot vs. cold
  storage; full fidelity for auth and sensitive operations; document any sampling/filtering for
  compliance.
- **"How would you add a new compliance rule?"** – Implement in policy-as-code, test, PR, merge;
  document which control it supports; pipeline picks it up on next run.
- **"What's the single biggest risk in this design?"** – e.g. "Unauthorized access to logs or
  tampering—we mitigate with access control, immutability, and auditing access to the log store."

**Optional second: IaC and compliance gates before deploy**

**How he might phrase it:** "Design a pipeline that takes infrastructure-as-code (e.g. Terraform)
and deploys to a customer environment. Before any deploy, the pipeline must check proposed changes
against a compliance policy (e.g. no public S3 buckets, encryption required). Walk me through the
flow, where policy lives, and how you'd add a new rule."

- **Why he's asking:** Do you think in pipelines and policy-as-code? (Aligns with his
  compliance-module patent.)
- **Strong:** Flow: IaC in version control → `terraform plan` → compliance engine (OPA, Checkov, or
  custom) against planned state → block on failure and report rule/resource → if pass, allow deploy
  → log plan + policy result for audit. Policy as code in repo; versioned and reviewed. New rule:
  implement, test, PR, merge, document which control (e.g. FedRAMP SC-28) it supports.
- **Red flag:** No policy-as-code; no audit trail of what was checked.

### Other possible technical or situational questions

- **"How would you run a Gen AI PoC in a government context?"** – Scope: narrow use case, clear
  success criteria, data boundary (where data lives, who sees it). Risk: PII/PHI/CUI handling, model
  provenance, no training on customer data without approval. Rollout: pilot users, feedback loop,
  off-ramp if it doesn't meet bar. One line: "Bounded PoC, then decide whether to harden for
  production."
- **"Describe a time you pushed back on a customer or stakeholder."** – What was asked, why it
  conflicted with policy/risk; how you explained risk and proposed an alternative; result:
  relationship preserved, safer solution.
- **"What's the hardest technical problem you've solved?"** – Pick one with production, scale, or
  compliance angle; have a **crisp 60-second version** (context, challenge, what you did, outcome).
- **"Where do you want to be in 3–5 years?"** – Align with FDE/technical leadership or
  customer-facing technical role. Do not say "I want to leave engineering."

1. **"How is Invisible thinking about infrastructure for agents in the enterprise, and where do FDEs

5. **"How does the team use patterns and IaC to stay consistent across customer environments?"** –

**What to do with his answer:** Listen; ask one brief follow-up if natural. Do not interrogate.

## 5. Objection handling

**Purpose:** When he challenges an answer or raises a concern, respond calmly and credibly—not
defensive or flustered.

**If he says or implies: "You don't have direct government/FedRAMP experience."**

- **Response template:** "That's fair—I haven't worked in a FedRAMP environment yet. What I have
  done is [compliance-relevant project or banking API]. For FedRAMP I'd [map to controls, evidence
  for assessor, pipeline checks]. I'm a fast learner and I've read up on the control families; I'd
  pair with your team and the customer's security folks early." Keep it short; don't over-apologize.

**If he says: "How would you handle a customer who wants something that's not compliant?"**

- **Response template:** "I'd explain the risk in plain language and the consequence—e.g. failing an
  audit or losing ATO. I'd propose an alternative that meets their goal within the guardrails. If
  they still pushed, I'd escalate and document so we don't take on risk we can't defend."

**If he asks a technical follow-up you don't know (e.g. specific NIST control number).**

- **Response template:** "I don't have that exact number in my head, but I know the principle—[e.g.
  audit events must be protected and retention must be configurable]. I'd look up the specific
  control and map our implementation to it for the assessor."

**If he challenges your system design ("What about X?" or "That would fail when…").**

- **Response template:** "Good catch—[acknowledge the gap]. We'd address that by [one or two
  concrete additions]. The trade-off would be [brief]. Does that align with how you've seen it
  done?"

**General rule:** Never get defensive. **"That's a valid concern,"** **"Good point,"** or **"I'd
want to validate that"**—then add one or two sentences that address the concern or show how you'd
find the answer.

hire you.

**Pick 2–3 themes that are true for you and weave into answers:**

- **Production-first mindset:** "I care as much about how we run and prove the system as about the

compliance awareness, and the ability to work in the customer's constraints—so Invisible can scale
in public sector without burning the customer or the team." Adapt to your words; use in the closing
or if he asks "why should we hire you?"

## 7. Closing strong and follow-up

**Purpose:** End on a high note and stay top-of-mind positively after the call.

**Last 2–3 minutes:**

- Thank him specifically: "Thanks for the time and for walking me through [one thing he said]."
- Reaffirm interest: "I'm really excited about the role and what Invisible is doing in public
  sector."
- **Ask next steps:** "What are the next steps on your side?" or "Is there anything else you need
  from me?"
- Optional: Use your differentiation line or "why FDE" in one sentence so the last thing he hears is
  your value prop.

**What not to do:** Don't oversell or beg. Don't ask about salary or benefits unless he brings it
up. Don't leave without clarity on next steps.

**Post-interview (within 24 hours):**

- Send a short thank-you email (3–5 sentences): thank him, reference **one specific topic** you
  discussed, reiterate interest, say you **look forward to next steps**.
- If you promised to send something (link, doc), send it the same day.
- Do not follow up repeatedly; one thank-you is enough unless they set an expectation (e.g. "We'll
  get back to you by Friday").

**If there's a delay:** After the stated timeline passes, one brief, professional follow-up: "Hi
Aaron, I wanted to check in on the next steps for the FDE role. I'm still very interested. Thanks."
Do not show frustration or send multiple chasers.
